Tips for Taxi Drivers

Explanatory Data Analysis (EDA)

A research has been conducted regarding the money taxi drivers receive in tip across different journeys. These journeys vary in the distance traveled, time duration, number of passengers, the time of day took place, taxi companies, and payment methods. Our goal is to investigate how these differences contribute to the amount of tips taxi drivers receive. The dataset contains 500 observations of 7 variables in total with no data missing.

The mean amount of tip taxi drivers receive in journeys is approximately 2.295 pounds, and the standard deviation is 1.124 pounds. We first analyze the relationships between our response variable tip and the three categorical covariates. The three boxplots shown below present the tip drivers receive when they work in different times of the day, for different companies, and pay through different payment methods. We can identify certain general patterns by observing these boxplots. In the first boxplot, it can be seen that drivers tend to receive more tips at night yet fewer tips in the morning. In the second boxplot, it can be identified that self-employed taxi drivers tend to receive slightly more tips than those drivers employed by the Green_Taxis and Red_Taxis companies. While in the last boxplot, we cannot yet identify the specific relationship between the tip drivers receive and the payment methods the passengers use as the three boxes are very close to each other. Therefore, further investigations will be required.

Now, we investigate the rest numeric covariates using scatterplots. The three scatterplots below demonstrate the relationships between the tip drivers receive and the mileage traveled during the taxi journey, the duration of the journey, and the number of passengers onboard. Observing the first and second scatterplots, the tips drivers receive increase as the taxi journey mileage and duration increase. Therefore, there appears to be a positive relationship between the tip amount and both the journey mileage and the trip duration. However, the third scatterplot is less informative and straightforward, indicating that further investigations may be required. But generally, there’s also a slight positive relationship between the tip amount and the number of passengers.

In conclusion, our initial findings suggest that all the 6 covariates – including mileage, duration, the time of the day of the journey, payment methods, the company the drivers belong to, and the number of passengers – may all affect the amount of tip the drivers receive. All variables should therefore be considered when building the statistical model for the money drivers received in tip.

Normality Check

  • As can be seen from the normal QQ-plot below, the data points on the two sides show significant deviations from the 45-degree line; we therefore conclude that the variable tip is not normally distributed.
  • By conducting a Shapiro-Wilk normality test, we get a p-value of 2.7142757^{-10} \(< 0.05\), which implies that the distribution of the variable tip is significantly different from the normal distribution, we reject the normality.
  • The lack of normality in the response variable tip does not raise any concerns about fitting a normal linear model, as it is not required.

Potential Model Improvement

Despite two possible improvements mentioned earlier – treating passengers covariate as categorical, and incorporating interactions between mileage and time of the day – there are some other approaches to potentially improve the model for tip further.

To begin with, since we observed a gap in the increment of the coefficients of the interactions of mileage and time, we can further break down time into more specific time period of the day. At present, the daytime is divided into morning, afternoon, and evening, while the entire night is treated as a single block. To address this, we could split the nighttime into two segments – early and late night – or even three by introducing a dusk period to complement the evening, which would balance the dispatch times more evenly.

Secondly, similar to the interaction implemented in model2, more interactions between covariates can be incorporated. For example, interactions between payment and time may have certain effects as it might be harder to hire a taxi at certain times of day – such as during the evening rush or or late at night – more people may opt to book and pay through an app.
